## üß† Core Concepts (The Deep Dive)

### 1. Structure & Types
All penicillins share a common nucleus: 6-APA (a thiazolidine ring fused to a Œ≤-lactam ring).
*   **Natural Penicillins:** Produced without precursors (e.g., Penicillin G, Penicillin V).
*   **Semi-synthetic Penicillins:** Created by adding specific side chains chemically to 6-APA (e.g., Ampicillin, Methicillin). These offer better acid stability and resistance to penicillinase.

### 2. Biosynthesis
Penicillin is synthesized in *P. chrysogenum* through a non-ribosomal process involving three amino acids:
1.  **L-valine**
2.  **L-cystine**
3.  **Œ±-aminoadipic acid (Œ±-AAA)**
They form a tripeptide intermediate which is then cyclized into the active antibiotic.

### 3. The Industrial Fermentation Process
Penicillin is produced via **Submerged Fermentation** in huge tanks (40,000‚Äì200,000 L).
*   **Microorganism:** High-yielding mutant strains of *P. chrysogenum*.
*   **Inoculum Production:** Spores are grown on wheat bran, then expanded into liquid tanks to reach high mycelial density.
*   **Medium:** 
    *   *Carbon:* Lactose (slow-release sugar prevents rapid acid buildup).
    *   *Nitrogen:* Corn-steep liquor (rich in amino acids).
    *   *Precursor:* Phenylacetic acid (for Penicillin G).
*   **Conditions:** Aerobic ($0.5-1.0$ vvm aeration), Temp ($25-27^\circ$C), pH controlled.

### 4. Extraction & Purification (The 3 Stages)
Penicillin is unstable at low pH, so extraction must be fast.
1.  **Separation:** Mycelium is removed using a **rotary vacuum filter**.
2.  **Solvent Extraction:** Liquid is acidified (pH 2.0) to make penicillin anionic, then extracted into organic solvents (e.g., amyl acetate).
3.  **Back Extraction:** Penicillin is moved back into water by raising the pH (7.0), then crystallized into salt form (e.g., Sodium Penicillin).

## ‚ö†Ô∏è Common Pitfalls
*   **Contamination:** If even a tiny amount of a bacterium producing **penicillinase** enters the tank, the entire batch is destroyed.
*   **Oxygen Transfer:** As the mold grows, the liquid becomes very thick (viscous), making it hard for oxygen to reach the cells. This is often the limiting factor for yield.
*   **Stability:** Penicillin G is destroyed by stomach acid, which is why it must be injected (unlike Penicillin V or Ampicillin).
