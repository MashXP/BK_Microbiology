## ðŸ§¼ Sterilization & Disinfection

-   **Sterilization**: The process of killing all bacteria, including vegetative cells and spores. The most common method is high-temperature treatment.
-   **Disinfection**: The process of killing pathogenic bacteria (non-spore-forming vegetative cells). This is usually done with chemicals, UV rays, boiling water, or steam.
-   **Antisepsis**: Disinfection applied directly to living tissue. The chemical used is called an antiseptic.

---

## ðŸ“ˆ Factors Affecting the Effectiveness of the Bactericidal Process

-   **The Rate of Microbial Death**: When subjected to heat treatment, bacteria are generally killed at a constant rate.
-   **The Number of Microbes**: The more microbes there are to begin with, the longer it takes to eliminate the entire population.
-   **Microbial Characteristics**:
    -   Spores are more difficult to destroy than vegetative cells.
    -   Different vegetative cells have different sensitivities to a certain physical or chemical agent.
-   **Environmental Influences**:
    -   Fats and proteins in the solution tend to protect bacteria.
    -   The lower the pH, the easier it is to kill bacteria.
-   **Time of Exposure**:
    -   The lower the temperature, the longer the time of exposure needed.
    -   It takes longer to kill spores than vegetative cells.

---

## ðŸ’¥ Actions of Microbial Control Agents

Microbial control agents act in two main ways:

1.  **Alteration of membrane permeability**: They alter the lipid and protein structures of the cell membrane, causing cellular contents to leak out.
2.  **Damage to proteins and nucleic acids**: They break the chemical bonds in proteins and nucleic acids, leading to denaturation and inactivation.

    -   **Direct flaming**: For inoculating loops.
    -   **Incineration**: To dispose of contaminated materials.

### 2. Filtration

-   The passage of a liquid or gas through a screen-like material with pores small enough to retain microorganisms.
-   Used to sterilize heat-sensitive materials like culture media, enzymes, vaccines, and antibiotic solutions.

### 3. Desiccation

-   Dehydration prevents bacteria from growing or reproducing, but they can remain viable for years.
-   This is the principle behind **lyophilization** (freeze-drying).

### 4. Osmotic Pressure

-   Creating a hypertonic environment (high salt or sugar concentration) causes water to leave the cell, inhibiting growth.

### 5. Radiation

-   The effect depends on wavelength, intensity, and duration of exposure.
-   **Ionizing Radiation** (< 1 nm wavelength; e.g., gamma rays, X-rays):
    -   Carries a lot of energy and penetrates deeply.
    -   Ionizes water into free radicals that damage DNA.
    -   Used to sterilize pharmaceuticals and medical instruments.
-   **Nonionizing Radiation** (> 1 nm wavelength; e.g., UV light):
    -   Does not penetrate deeply.
    -   UV light is absorbed by DNA, causing mutations.
    -   Causes bonds to form between adjacent thymine bases (thymine dimers), leading to errors in DNA replication.
    -   Used to disinfect surfaces, air, and water.
-   **Microwaves**: Kill pathogenic bacterial cells through heat.

## ðŸ§ª Chemical Methods of Microbial Control

-   **Phenol and Phenolics**: Damage the plasma membrane.
    -   **Triclosan**: Specifically inhibits an enzyme needed for lipid biosynthesis.
    -   *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* is resistant to triclosan.
-   **Chlorhexidine**: Intervenes with membrane structure.
-   **Essential Oils**: (e.g., peppermint, pine oil) Contain phenolics (carvacrol) and terpenes (limonene) that have antimicrobial action.
-   **Iodine (Iâ‚‚)**: Impairs protein synthesis and alters cell membranes. Used for skin disinfection.
    -   **Iodophor (e.g., Betadine/Povidone-iodine)**: A combination of iodine and an organic molecule that releases iodine slowly.
-   **Chlorine (Clâ‚‚)**: Forms hypochlorous acid (HOCl), a strong oxidizing agent that damages enzymes.
-   **Alcohols** (ethanol, isopropanol): Denature proteins and dissolve lipids. Not effective against endospores.
-   **Heavy Metals** (Ag, Hg, Cu): Denature proteins by interacting with sulfhydryl groups.
-   **Surface-Active Agents**:
    -   **Soaps and Detergents**: Mechanical removal of microbes.
    -   **Quaternary Ammonium Compounds (Quats)**: Positively charged cations that disrupt the cell membrane.
        -   Strongly bactericidal against Gram-positive bacteria; less active against Gram-negative bacteria due to their outer membrane, which acts as a barrier and is less permeable to these compounds.
-   **Chemical Food Preservatives**:
    -   **Organic Acids** (sorbic acid, benzoic acid): Inhibit metabolism.
    -   **Nitrates/Nitrites**: Inhibit iron-containing enzymes in anaerobic bacteria.
-   **Antibiotics**:
    -   **Nisin**: A bacteriocin that inhibits endospore-forming bacteria in cheese.
    -   **Natamycin**: An antifungal antibiotic used in cheese.
-   **Aldehydes** (formaldehyde, glutaraldehyde): Inactivate proteins by cross-linking functional groups.
    -   **Formalin** (37% formaldehyde solution) is used to preserve specimens and inactivate viruses in vaccines.
    -   **Glutaraldehyde** is less irritating and more effective than formaldehyde, used to disinfect hospital instruments.
-   **Hydrogen Peroxide (Hâ‚‚Oâ‚‚)**: Kills microbes through oxidation.
