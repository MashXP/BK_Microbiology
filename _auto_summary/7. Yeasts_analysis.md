    *   **(Fermentation end-products):** In fermentation, the pyruvic acid and the electrons carried by NADH from glycolysis are incorporated into fermentation end-products.

### Page 21

**Description of Content:**
This page details the "Preparatory stage" of glycolysis, illustrating the initial steps where glucose is activated and split.

*   **Header:** "Preparatory stage"
*   **Main Diagram (Left):** A biochemical pathway showing the conversion of glucose into two 3-carbon molecules.
    *   It starts with a 6-carbon molecule labeled "Glucose" (represented by 6 interconnected blue circles).
    *   **Step 1:** Glucose undergoes phosphorylation. One molecule of ATP (shown as a yellow starburst labeled "ATP") is consumed, converting to ADP, and a phosphate group (P, a small red circle) is added to glucose, forming "Glucose 6-phosphate".
    *   **Step 2:** Glucose 6-phosphate rearranges to "Fructose 6-phosphate" (still a 6-carbon molecule).
    *   **Step 3:** Fructose 6-phosphate undergoes another phosphorylation. A second ATP molecule is consumed, becoming ADP, and another phosphate group is added, resulting in "Fructose 1,6-diphosphate" (a 6-carbon molecule with two phosphate groups).
    *   **Step 4:** Fructose 1,6-diphosphate is cleaved (split) into two distinct 3-carbon molecules: "Dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP)" and "Glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate (GP)". Both have one phosphate group.
    *   **Step 5:** Dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP) is shown to be readily converted to Glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate (GP) via a reversible reaction (indicated by double-headed arrows). This means both resulting 3-carbon molecules will proceed as GP.
*   **Inset Diagram (Top Right):** A smaller diagram provides an overview of cellular respiration and fermentation.
    *   It shows "RESPIRATION" and "FERMENTATION" as major pathways.
    *   "Glycolysis" is depicted at the top, converting "Glucose" to "Pyruvic acid (or derivatives)".
    *   Pyruvic acid can then enter the "Krebs cycle" (via "Acetyl CoA") or go into "Fermentation".
    *   The "Krebs cycle" feeds into the "Electron transport chain and chemiosmosis".
    *   Inputs/outputs like NAD+, NADH, FADH2, ATP, CO2 are visible.
*   **Explanatory Text (Right, Numbered Points):** These points correspond to the numbered steps in the main diagram.
    *   **1** Glucose enters the cell and is phosphorylated. A molecule of ATP is invested. The product is glucose 6-phosphate.
    *   **2** Glucose 6-phosphate is rearranged to form fructose 6-phosphate.
    *   **3** The P from another ATP is used to produce fructose 1,6-diphosphate, still a six-carbon compound. (Note the total investment of two ATP molecules up to this point.)
    *   **4** An enzyme cleaves (splits) the sugar into two three-carbon molecules: dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP) and glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate (GP).
    *   **5** DHAP is readily converted to GP (the reverse action may also occur).

### Page 22

**Description of Content:**
This page continues the glycolysis pathway, detailing the "Energy-conserving stage" where ATP and NADH are produced.

*   **Header:** "Energy-conserving stage"
*   **Figure Caption:** "Figure 5.12 An outline of the reactions of glycolysis (Embden-Meyerhof pathway). The inset indicates the relationship of glycolysis to the overall processes of respiration and fermentation. A more detailed version of glycolysis is presented in Figure A.2 in Appendix A."
*   **Main Diagram (Left):** A biochemical pathway showing the energy-yielding steps of glycolysis.
    *   It begins with "2 Glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate (GP)" molecules (from Step 5 on Page 1, indicating that the single glucose molecule has been split into two 3-carbon units).
    *   **Step 6:** Each GP molecule is oxidized and phosphorylated. 2 NAD+ are reduced to 2 NADH (shown as a purple oval labeled "NADH"), and two inorganic phosphates (Pi) are added, resulting in "2 1,3-diphosphoglyceric acid".
    *   **Step 7:** Substrate-level phosphorylation occurs. The phosphate group from each 1,3-diphosphoglyceric acid is transferred to ADP, producing 2 ATP molecules (yellow starburst labeled "ATP") and "2 3-phosphoglyceric acid". This step repays the initial ATP investment.
    *   **Step 8:** Each 3-phosphoglyceric acid is rearranged to "2 2-phosphoglyceric acid".
    *   **Step 9:** Each 2-phosphoglyceric acid undergoes dehydration, releasing "2 H2O" molecules (blue H2O droplet) and forming "2 Phosphoenolpyruvic acid (PEP)".
    *   **Step 10:** Another substrate-level phosphorylation. The phosphate group from each PEP is transferred to ADP, producing "2 ATP" molecules (yellow starburst) and "2 Pyruvic acid" (the final 3-carbon product of glycolysis).
*   **Inset Diagram (Top Left):** The same overview of respiration and fermentation as Page 1, with "Glycolysis" highlighted.
*   **Explanatory Text (Right, Numbered Points):** These points correspond to the numbered steps in the main diagram.
    *   **6** The next enzyme converts each GP to another three-carbon compound, 1,3-diphosphoglyceric acid. Because each DHAP molecule can be converted to GP and each GP to 1,3-diphosphoglyceric acid, the result is two molecules of 1,3-diphosphoglyceric acid for each initial molecule of glucose. GP is oxidized by the transfer of two hydrogen atoms to NAD+ to form NADH. The enzyme couples this reaction with the creation of a high-energy bond between the sugar and a P. The three-carbon sugar now has two P groups.
    *   **7** The high-energy P is moved to ADP, forming ATP, the first ATP production of glycolysis. (Since the sugar splitting in step 4, all products are doubled. Therefore, this step actually repays the earlier investment of two ATP molecules.)
    *   **8** An enzyme relocates the remaining P of 3-phosphoglyceric acid to form 2-phosphoglyceric acid in preparation for the next step.
    *   **9** By the loss of a water molecule, 2-phosphoglyceric acid is converted to phosphoenolpyruvic acid (PEP). In the process, the phosphate bond is upgraded to a high-energy bond.
    *   **10** This high-energy P is transferred from PEP to ADP, forming ATP. For each initial glucose molecule, the result of this step is two molecules of ATP and two molecules of a three-carbon compound called pyruvic acid.

### Page 47

*   **Header:** "Yeast cell quantification"
*   **Diagram:** A side-by-side comparison of two common methods for quantifying microbial populations: (a) The pour plate method and (b) The spread plate method.
    *   **(a) The pour plate method (Green Section):**
        *   **Step 1:** Shows an empty Petri dish and a flask containing a liquid culture. An arrow indicates "Inoculate empty plate."
        *   **Step 2:** Shows the empty plate with melted nutrient agar being poured into it. The liquid inoculum (1.0 or 0.1 ml) is shown in a test tube. "Add melted nutrient agar."
        *   **Step 3:** Shows the plate being swirled to mix the inoculum with the agar. "Swirl to mix."
        *   **Step 4:** Shows a solidified plate with colonies growing on and within the solidified agar medium. A magnified view shows individual colonies. "Colonies grow on and in solidified medium."
    *   **(b) The spread plate method (Purple Section):**
        *   **Step 1:** Shows a Petri dish containing solid agar medium. A test tube containing a "Bacterial dilution" is shown with "0.1 ml" indicated. "Inoculate plate containing solid medium."
        *   **Step 2:** Shows a pipette spreading the inoculum across the surface of the agar. A red arrow indicates the spreading motion. "Spread inoculum over surface evenly."
        *   **Step 3:** Shows a solidified plate with colonies growing only on the surface of the agar medium. A magnified view shows individual colonies. "Colonies grow only on surface of medium."

**Analysis:** This page illustrates two common plating techniques used for quantifying viable yeast (or bacterial) cells. The pour plate method involves mixing the inoculum with molten agar before it solidifies, allowing colonies to grow both on and within the agar. The spread plate method involves spreading a small volume of inoculum onto the surface of pre-solidified agar, leading to colonies growing only on the surface. Both methods rely on the formation of distinct colonies from individual cells (or clumps of cells) and are used to determine the number of viable cells per unit volume in the original sample.
