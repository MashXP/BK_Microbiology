### Page 23
**Headers:** Same as Page 2.

**Content:**
*   **Superoxide radicals ($O^{2-}$), or superoxide anions:** These are formed in small amounts during normal respiration when oxygen is used as a final electron acceptor to form water.
*   **Neutralization:** Organisms growing in atmospheric oxygen must produce the enzyme **superoxide dismutase (SOD)** to neutralize them. Even obligate anaerobes form some superoxide radicals in the presence of oxygen, which are toxic to cellular components.
*   **Toxicity Mechanism:** Their toxicity stems from instability, causing them to steal electrons from neighboring molecules, creating a chain reaction of radical formation.

### Page 24
This page provides the chemical equations for the enzymes that neutralize reactive oxygen species.

**Equations:**
1.  **Superoxide Dismutase (SOD):** $O_2^- + O_2^- + 2 H^+ \rightarrow H_2O_2 + O_2$. (Converts superoxide radicals into hydrogen peroxide and oxygen).
2.  **Catalase:** $2 H_2O_2 \rightarrow 2 H_2O + O_2$. (Converts hydrogen peroxide into water and oxygen).
3.  **Peroxidase:** $H_2O_2 + 2 H^+ \rightarrow 2 H_2O$. (Converts hydrogen peroxide and hydrogen ions into water).

*   **Streak plate method:** This indicates the specific technique being discussed. It is written in red font and preceded by a diamond-shaped bullet point.

**Visual Content:**
The slide features two illustrations labeled (a) and (b), which demonstrate the process and result of the streak plate method on a Petri dish (agar plate).

*   **Diagram (a):** This is a schematic diagram illustrating the physical action of streaking a sample across an agar surface to achieve isolation.
    *   The diagram shows a circular Petri dish with four distinct areas marked with numbers **1, 2, 3, and 4**, representing the sequence of quadrants.
    *   **Quadrant 1:** Shows dense, zigzag yellow lines where the initial sample is concentrated.
    *   **Quadrants 2, 3, and 4:** Show subsequent streaking patterns. In each step, the inoculating loop is used to pull a small amount of the sample from the previous quadrant and spread it into a new area. Arrowheads indicate the direction of movement.
    *   The goal shown here is the systematic dilution of the bacterial sample across the surface of the medium.

*   **Photograph (b):** This is a real-life image of an agar plate after it has been streaked and incubated, showing actual bacterial growth.
    *   The growth follows the pattern described in diagram (a). 
    *   In the first quadrant (top left), there is a heavy, confluent mass of red-orange bacteria.
    *   As the streaking continues into the other quadrants, the bacteria become more diluted.
    *   Two black lines point to individual, distinct circular spots on the agar, which are labeled as **"Colonies."** These individual colonies represent "pure cultures" because each one originated from a single bacterial cell or a small group of identical cells.
    *   The photograph clearly shows two different types of bacteria present: one that forms large red-orange masses and another that forms smaller, distinct white colonies. This visualizes how the streak plate method is used to separate different species from a mixed sample.
